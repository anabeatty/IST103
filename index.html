Discover the history of Carimbó, the typical dance of Pará

Carimbó also called Samba de roda do Marajó or Pau e Corda, is a typical dance from the north of Brazil, which
brings together joy and rhythm.

Dancing is one of the best ways to express joy, unite entire communities and feel that there, in dance, is possible for harmony. From north to south, from east to west of Brazil, the dance is
present in its different forms, such as carimbó, a typical dance from the northern region of the country.

Originating in the state of Pará, the carimbó was born from the farmers' desire to dance, who as soon as they finished work at the end of the day, danced to the sound of the curimbó, the
main instrument of Pará dance.

The name carimbó, or curimbó, comes from Tupi language, where curi means stick and m’bó means holed, which in Portuguese means “stick that produces the sound”. The curimbó
instrument, whose name is similar to the name of the dance, is played with the legs that embrace it to produce sound.

The typical dance from northeastern Pará, as well as other types of Brazilian dances, received indigenous, African and European influence. The mixture then made the carimbó, also called
Samba de roda do Marajó or Pau e Corda, one of the main artistic representations of the people of Pará.

Origin of the Carimbó

In short, in addition to dance and rhythm, carimbó also constitutes an identity. In this sense, with the mixture of various peoples, it is possible to perceive the heritage of black people in
the dancing, which is also present in the fast drumming of the banjo sound. With European influence, the typical dance of Pará includes the flute, saxophone and clarinet, in addition to the
formation of couples, who twirl according to the sound of the carimbó. This reference even came from Portuguese colonization.

Despite the various references, it is not possible to say exactly where the carimbó was actually born. However, some historians, such as Agripino da Conceição, claim that the origin
of the dance came from Marapanim, more specifically in the community of Maranhãozinho. In fact, the Marapanim region is where you can find most groups that cultivate the tradition
of typical Pará dance. There, too, is where the “Carimbó de Marapanim Festival” takes place every year.

Furthermore, it is also believed that the carimbó do Pará is a typical dance that was brought to Brazil by African slaves. Over the years, the dance would have incorporated influences
from other peoples, such as indigenous people and Europeans. Thus, the custom of dancing would have arisen from the habit of farmers and fishermen, whenever they finished working,
getting together in groups to dance to the rhythm of the curimbó drum.

Types of Carimbó

At the beginning of the dance manifestation, as the state of Pará is very large, the carimbó was divided into three groups: carimbó praieiro, carimbó pastoral and carimbó rural. In addition
to the division according to the state's regions, two stamp currents were also adopted: the traditional and the modern style. Thus, in the traditional style, the musical structure referring to
Marapaniense was maintained and its main representative is the singer Verequete. On the other hand, the modern style has a structure that mixes more modern concepts of rhythm, being
represented by the singer Pinduca.

At the time when the rhythm was divided into two currents, there was an intense rivalry between those who defended tradition and modernity. However, the styles that make up the
carimbó vary depending on the type of activity performed in each region of Pará. Thus, the main difference between the rhythms is in the rhymes that make up the carimbó.

Characteristics of the dance

Carimbó is a dance that brings together happy people, who dance without costumes and like to feel their bare feet on the dirt floor. The costumes and choreography are certainly the
characteristics that most distinguish carimbó dancers, as well as the type of playing of the instruments and drums. To produce the sound of typical Pará dance, instruments cannot be
missing. The main ones used are two drums, accompanied by a flute, the reco-reco, the banjo, the ganzá and a tambourine.

In the dance, women decorate their heads with flowers, and wear plenty of accessories around their necks and wrists, which make a sound to the rhythm of the drum. Furthermore,
women still wear very colorful and voluminous skirts, which give the feeling of a lot of movement when dancing. Men, on the other hand, wear white pants, usually with rolled-up hems,
a legacy inherited from their black ancestors, who rolled up the hems of their pants to work on large mills. It is also the men who start the choreography and invite their partners to join
the dance, to the sound of clapping.

Over time, the carimbó gained a lot of notoriety in other regions of Brazil, being linked to various religious celebrations. One of the celebrations is the party that takes place every
year in the municipality of Bragança, in honor of Santo Benedito. In this sense, due to its connection with religious festivities, the carimbó received, in 2014, the title of Intangible
Cultural Heritage of Brazil.

Prominent carimbó artists and recognition

In summary, carimbó is a typical dance from the northeast of Pará, but which was spread to other regions of Brazil through various artists, such as the band Calypso. In addition to
the band, it is also possible to highlight other artists who contributed to the spread of dance across Brazilian states, for example:

Pinduca from the group “Pinduca e banda” (Belém);
Aroldo from the group “Revelação do Zimba (Salinópolis);
Ticó from the group “Quentes da Madrugada” (Santarém Novo);
Lico from the group “Beija-Flor” (Vigia);
Moacir from the “Filhos de Maiandeua” Group (Maracanã);
Mário from the group “Japiim” (Marapanim);
Elias from the group “Raios de Luz” (Curuçá);
Verequete and Manoel from the group “O Uirapurí” (Belém);
Amélia from the group “Cruzeirinho” (Soure/Marajó);
Bigica from the group “Sereia do Mar” (Marapanim).

In summary, Carimbó Municipal Day is celebrated in Pará, on August 26th. The date was chosen in honor of the birth date of Mestre Verequete, a musician known as the
King of Carimbó. In 2014, due to the popularization of the Pará dance, the carimbó was declared an Intangible Cultural Heritage of Brazil by the Cultural Heritage Advisory Council.
The decision, at the time, was unanimous. Finally, a year later, in 2015, the typical dance of Pará received the title of Cultural Heritage of Brazil from the National Historical and
Artistic Heritage Institute (IPHAN).
